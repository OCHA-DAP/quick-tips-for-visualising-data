Why We Visualise Data

01

We Visualise to Explore & Explain

We use visualisastion to explore data, to uncover different relationships and to discover the most meaningful information to highlight or analyse further.

We use visualistion to explain what we are seeing in the data. Explanatory visualisation can be used to inform, persuade and engage an audience. This is the type of visualisation we are going to focus on here.

01

How to Explain with Visualisation

Explanatory data visualisation requires a defined audience and clear objective. Before you start to create your visualisation, ask yourself the following:

  • What am I trying to say? The message that you are trying to convey should guide the design choices that you make.
  • Who am I trying to say it to? Thinking about who your audience is and how they will interact with and use your visualisation will help focus your final product.

02

Common COVID-19 Data Points

The following are a few common figures in COVID-19 datasets:

  1. Active Cases includes the number of confirmed cases (and suspected cases where that is available) for a given timeframe minus those people who have died or recovered from COVID-19. (Also known as "prevalence").
  2. New Cases includes the number of confirmed cases (and suspected cases where that is available) for a given timeframe. (Also known as “incidence").
  3. Cumulative Cases are the total confirmed cases up to a given point in time, without subtracting people who have recovered or died. These numbers over time only get larger and larger as new cases are added.

Designing Responsibly

03

Design Considerations for Responsible Visualisations

The way we communicate about the COVID-19 pandemic has the potential to shape our audience's perception of risk and safety.

The design choices we make influence how our audience interprets our underlying data. This is because through our design we choose to emphasise certain things while potentially obscuring others. While there are no hard and fast rules for making design choices, the following are a few considerations to think about when creating COVID-19 data visualisations.

03

Choose Colours Carefully

We associate red with danger. A map full of overlapping red circles will communicate danger and as a result may distract from other messages. Consider choosing neutral colours when creating COVID-19 visualisations.

Source World Health Organisation Dashboard

Consider Using a Logarithmic Scale

Plotting exponential growth on a linear scale can end up obscuring important details such as the rate of growth. 03

Linear and Logarithmic Scales

The most common form of a line-graph uses a linear scale. Along the Y axis, the numbers progress in a linear form – 1, 2, 3, or 10, 20, 30, etc.

On a logarithmic scale, numbers on the Y-axis don’t increase in equal increments. Each interval increases by a set factor – often 10 but could be a factor of 3 or 350 or 3,500, etc.

A logarithmic scale prevents large numbers from skewing a whole graph and is good for measuring rates of change, particularly rates of growth.

03

RELATIVE and ABSOLUTE Values

It is important to explore different ways to represent the data you are visualising. For example, when comparing across countries or regions, consider how your visualisation will change based on your choice to use absolute values (total # of confirmed cases) verse relative values (# of confirmed cases per X thousand people).

In their purest sense an absolute value is a value compared to nothing, and a relative value is created when two absolute values are compared to each other. Relative values are often presented as percentages, proportions, rates, etc., and are good for providing a reference for readers to judge magnitude and to compare magnitude across different categories.

The choice to use one or the other depends on the message that you are trying to convey.

03

Use Case for Relative COVID-19 Numbers

A choropleth map is an example of a visualisation that works best with relative numbers because relative numbers provide the context to sensibly compare magnitude across countries or regions. In thr case of COVID-19, population size is relevant for comparing the magnitude of the outbreak at any given snapship in time.

Source Our World in Data
Press down for more information

03

Use Case for Absolute COVID-19 Numbers

However, many designers of COVID-19 logarithmic line graphs are choosing to use absolute numbers, why? The emphasis when using a logarithmic scale is the rate of growth, or trajectory, rather than the magnitude. Adjustments for population size would push smaller countries higher up on the graph making their outbreaks appear more severe and larger countries down, making theirs appear less severe. It's a judgement call.

Source John Burn-Murdoch at the Financial Times

03

Communicate Uncertainty

There is a great deal of uncertainty around COVID-19 and this is present in the data. Try to find ways to convey that uncertainty through your visualisation. For example, adding the simple phrase ‘we know of X cases’ helps to convey the critical message that the data we have is incomplete.

Source Lisa Charlotte Rost

Choose the Right Visualisation

04

Relationship Types

Choosing the right visualisations can feel overwhelming. There are a lot of options out there. The best way to narrow it down is to identify the relationship between the variables that you want to highlight.

In this section, we will explore the following relationship types and discuss good chart options for each:

  1. Change Over Time
  2. Comparison
  3. Distribution
  4. Part of a Whole
  5. Geospatial

04

Change Over Time

If you have time series data, a common relationship to visualise is change over time. Make sure to plot the timeline on the X-axis. The following charts are powerful options for helping to illustrate trends over time.

04

Line Graph

A line graph will work for most time series data. Line charts are great for visualising trends, which is one of the reasons they are a go to choice for COVID-19 visualisations. Adding visual markers for important events, e.g, when stay at home orders went into effect, is an easy way to add context.

Source: Our World in Data
Press down to see more examples.

04

Bar Chart

Bar charts for time series work best when dealing with distinct points in time such as days, months or years, e.g., - the number of confirmed cases reported daily.

Source: Worldometer
Press down to see more examples

04

Stacked Area Graph

Stacked area graphs are useful for comparing multiple data series changing over time. The values of each group are displayed on top of each other, which allows you to visualise the change of the total value, e.g, the number of confirmed COVID-19 cases globally, and how the values are evolving in component groups, e.g., region.

Source: Our World in Data

04

Magnitude & Ranking

Visualisations will often use size, length and position to help viewers explore differences and similarities between categories and data series.

Source Andy Kriebel & The Financial Times
Press down to learn more

04

Ordered Bar Graph

Ordered bar and column charts are excellent for showing position and comparing, e.g., a list of COVID-19 cases by country. Fun fact: a 'bar chart' with vertical bars is called a column chart.

Source: Our World in Data on HDX

04

Distribution

Distribution visualisations are often used to explore data as they show the values in the dataset and how often they occur. The shape (or the skew) of a distribution can be a memorable way of highlighting the lack of uniformity or equality in the data.

Source Andy Kriebel & The Financial Times
Press down to learn more

04

Histogram

Histograms are powerful visual representations of the spread of the data. They show where values are concentrated, what the extremes are and whether there are any gaps . They may look similarly to a vertical bar chart but are different in an important way. The horizontal (x) axis is numerical, not categorical.

Source: Springer

04

Part of a Whole

These charts are for when you want to show how various parts of the data comprise the whole. While useful with a small number of categories they can quickly become difficult to read. If you are mainly interested in comparing the size of the parts, often a simple bar chart will be easier to read.

Source Andy Kriebel & The Financial Times
Press down to learn more

04

Donut Charts

The donut chart has become increasing popular. It is similar to the popular pie chart but the centre can be a good way of making space to include additional information about the data (e.g., total). Keep the number of categories represented reasonable - the more categories, the more difficult the chart will be to read.

Source: OCHA Financial Tracking Services
Press down to see more chart options.

04

Proportional Stacked Bar

The proportional stacked bar is another simple way of showing part-to-whole relationship. This chart is espcially when comparing variables across groups or at different points in time.

Source:Eleonora Nazander

04

Geospatial

Use only when precise locations or geographical patterns in data are a crucial part of your message. When the interesting patterns in your data are not geographic patterns a map may end up distracting from your message. Just because it can be mapped, does not mean it must be mapped.

Source Andy Kriebel & The Financial Times
