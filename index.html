Why Do We Visualise Data?

01

We Visualise to Explore

We visualise data to explore the data, uncover different relationships and determine the most meaningful information to highlight or analyse further. The audience for this type of visualisation is often the analyst. This type of visualisation allows the analyst to spot trends or outliers and develop questions for further exploration. This is an age old tradition - learn about how exploratory data visualisation changed how we understood the spread of cholera way back in 1874.

02

We Visualise To Explain

We visualise data to explain a phenomenon we are seeing in the data. Explanatory data visualisation can be used to inform, persuade and engage an audience but critically, this type of visualisation requires a well defined audience and a clear objective. This is the type of visualisation we are going to be focussed on here.

Understand the Data

03

Epistomological Corner

Before digging into COVID-19 data visualisation, it is important to spend a bit of time understanding what can – and what cannot - be said based on this data. It is best practice to understand what data is contained in a dataset, what that data represents, and in this case a few basic concepts around epistemological data.

Design Reponsibly in a Crisis

03

Designing Responsbily

The way that we communicate about this epidemic has the potential to shape their view of risk and safety.

The design choices we make will influence how our audience interprets our underlying data. There are no hard and fast rules for making responsible design choices. The following are a few things to consider when creating COVID-19 data visualisation.

04

Ask yourself two questions

  1. What am I trying to say?
  2. Who am I trying to say it to?

06

Choose Colours Carefully

We associate red with danger. A map full of a overlapping red circles, or ever scarier shades of red, will communicate the danger but may distract from from other messages. Consider choosing more neutral colours when creating COVID-19 visualisations.

Source World Health Orgasnisation Dashboard

07

Be Careful with Absolute Numbers

When comparing across counrties, explore the differences in the visualisation using absolute (total # confirmed cases) and relative (# of confirmed cases per x thousand people). In the map on the left, we are looking at the total number of cases whereas the map on the right shows the number of cases per 100,000.

Source Kenneth Field

08

Pay Attention when cropping your axis

Cropping an axis can remove important context from the visual. In the case below (left), the cropped axis may unintentionally make the fatality rate seems worse than it is by obscuring the visual power of the percentage of people who survive.

Source Andy Cotgreave

09

Consider Using a Logarithmic Scale

When visualising a variable growing exponential, a linear scale can obscure important details. For example, the US and Italy look like they are on similar trajectories on the linear scale whereas it is clear with the logarithmic scale that COVID-19 cases are growing faster in the US.

Source Kenneth Chang

10

Embrace Complexity

There is a great deal of uncertainty around COVID-19 generally - this uncertainty does not go away in the data. Try to find ways to convey that uncertainty through your visualisation. For example, adding the simple phrase ‘we know of x cases’ helps to convey the critical message that the data we have is incomplete.

Source Lisa Charlotte Rost
